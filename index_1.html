Te anga · The framework

Ngā pō o te marama

The sequence of nights follows the maramataka widely shared through wānanga and publications, used across Tāmaki Makaurau and the upper North Island. Each night keeps its full traditional name, because the names carry mātauranga that a collapsed label cannot. Read as a leadership rhythm, the cycle tells us when to observe, when to gather, when to act, when to celebrate, and when to rest.

He kupu whakamārama. The names here are only one part of the maramataka. As many rangatira teach, the real practice comes from observing the tohu in your own environment: the fish, the birds, the plants, the tides, the winds. Iwi and hapū hold their own maramataka, shaped by their whenua, moana, and mātauranga, and those regional forms take precedence in their own rohe.

Dates are derived from astronomical new moon times in Aotearoa New Zealand. When a lunar month holds twenty-nine nights rather than thirty, Ōmutu rests and Mutuwhenua closes the month. This calendar is offered as a guide, not a prescription.

The maramataka is a taonga handed down through generations of careful observation. This site offers it as a leadership discipline, so that the way we lead, decide, and rest moves in rhythm with te taiao rather than against it.
